Hi, Amru Here!

I’m a graduate student in the Physics Dept. — NCHU, intrigued by how quantum mechanics reshapes our understanding of information. My research explores quantum correlations in multipartite systems and device-independent scenarios, using semidefinite programming to uncover hidden quantum properties that were once thought impossible.

Outside the world of quantum weirdness, I spend time making questionable progress on the violin and piano, capturing photons through lenses, and collecting books with the noble intention of reading them all (someday). Semidefinite programs may bend my brain, but badminton usually helps snap it back.

About

Hi, I'm Muhammad Amru Al-Amin, a graduate student in the Department of Physics at National Chung Hsing University in Taiwan, fortunate to be advised by Shin-Liang Chen. My research broadly focuses on quantum information theory, with a particular interest in fundamental quantum correlations, often motivated by their applications in a device-independent framework.

Currently, I'm a member of the NCHU Quantum Information Group, working on problems related to Bell's nonlocality and measurement incompatibility in multipartite systems (quantum networks), using semidefinite programming and its relaxations. In 2024, I completed my master’s degree in this group with a thesis on bounding quantum correlations in bilocal networks via semidefinite relaxations .

I earned my undergraduate degree in the Department of Physics at Institut Teknologi Sepuluh Nopember in Indonesia, where I worked with Agus Purwanto on quantum computing. My undergraduate thesis focused on applying the Bernstein-Vazirani algorithm to a nuclear magnetic resonance-based quantum computer model, completed in 2021.

I also go by the (formal) Mandarin name 何慕安 (Hé Mù'ān), even though I’m not ethnically Chinese — a name my teacher chose for me, appreciating its elegance and meaningful symbolism. Outside of research, I make (sometimes half-hearted) attempts to find time for my personal passions: capturing moody photographs that often remain buried in my digital archives, accumulating books I swear I’ll read one day, playing badminton with a lot of enthusiasm but little finesse, and producing sounds on the violin and piano that I’d prefer to call “noises” rather than “melodies.” I’m also struggling (but still trying!) to learn Traditional Chinese (繁體中文) — a beautiful script that constantly challenges both my patience and my handwriting on regular basis.
